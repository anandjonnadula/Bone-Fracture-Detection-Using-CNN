Understanding Bone Fractures

The twelve fracture categories this system can recognize, and what they mean. Severity shown is the general clinical tendency — individual cases vary.

Mild

Hairline (Stress) Fracture

A thin crack that does not go all the way through the bone, usually caused by repetitive stress or overuse. Often barely visible on X-rays and treated with rest and activity modification.

Mild

Greenstick Fracture

The bone bends and cracks on one side without breaking completely — like snapping a green twig. Most common in children, whose bones are softer and more flexible.

Moderate

Avulsion Fracture

A small fragment of bone is pulled away by a tendon or ligament, typically during sudden forceful muscle contraction — common in athletes around the ankle, hip and pelvis.

Moderate

Spiral Fracture

A break that spirals around the bone shaft, caused by a twisting force — for example, a planted foot with a rotating body. Common in the tibia.

Moderate

Oblique Fracture

A clean break running diagonally across the bone shaft, usually from an angled impact. Stability depends on the angle and displacement of the fragments.

Moderate

Compression (Crush) Fracture

The bone is crushed or collapses on itself — most often in the vertebrae of the spine, frequently associated with osteoporosis or axial-load injuries.

Moderate

Impacted Fracture

The broken ends of the bone are driven into each other by the force of the injury, such as bracing a fall with an outstretched arm.

Severe

Comminuted Fracture

The bone shatters into three or more fragments, typically from high-energy trauma such as vehicle collisions. Usually requires surgical fixation.

Severe

Fracture Dislocation

A fracture occurring together with a dislocated joint — the bone breaks near a joint whose surfaces are also forced out of alignment. A surgical emergency in many cases.

Severe

Intra-articular Fracture

The fracture line extends into the joint surface itself. Requires precise anatomical reduction to avoid long-term arthritis and loss of joint function.

Severe

Longitudinal Fracture

A break that runs along the long axis of the bone. Often produced by axial compression forces and can be subtle on standard views.

Severe

Pathological Fracture

A break through bone weakened by underlying disease — osteoporosis, tumors, or infection — often after minimal trauma. The underlying condition must be treated too.

When to seek help

Seek immediate medical attention for any suspected fracture with visible deformity, inability to bear weight, numbness, severe swelling, or an open wound near the injury. This tool is an educational demonstration — it cannot rule out a fracture.
